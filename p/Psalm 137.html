Psalm 137

1. By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down and wept, when we remembered Zion.

2. On the willows there we hung up our lyres.

3. For there our captors required of us songs, and our tormentors, mirth, saying, "Sing us one of the songs of Zion!"

4. How shall we sing the Lord's song in a foreign land?

5. If I forget you, O Jerusalem, let my right hand forget its skill!

6. Let my tongue stick to the roof of my mouth, if I do not remember you, if I do not set Jerusalem above my highest joy!

7. Remember, O Lord, against the Edomites the day of Jerusalem, how they said, "Lay it bare, lay it bare, down to its foundations!"

8. O daughter of Babylon, doomed to be destroyed, blessed shall he be who repays you with what you have done to us!

9. Blessed shall he be who takes your little ones and dashes them against the rock!

Commentary

Psalm 137 is a poignant psalm that expresses the deep sorrow and longing of the Israelites during their exile in Babylon. It reflects their grief and yearning for Jerusalem, their beloved city, as well as their desire for justice against their oppressors.

The psalm begins with a vivid scene by the rivers of Babylon, where the exiled Israelites sat down and wept, remembering Zion (Jerusalem). They hung up their lyres, musical instruments used for praise and worship, indicating their loss of joy and inability to sing in their current state of captivity.

The captors and tormentors of the Israelites demanded songs of Zion, mocking their longing for their homeland. The psalmist questions how they could sing the Lord's song in a foreign land, where their hearts were filled with sorrow and their surroundings were hostile to their faith.

The psalmist declares their unwavering commitment to Jerusalem and their refusal to forget it. They express a powerful oath, stating that if they were to forget Jerusalem, their right hand should forget its skill and their tongue should stick to the roof of their mouth. Jerusalem holds a higher place of honor and joy in their hearts than anything else.

In verse 7, the psalmist calls upon the Lord to remember the actions of the Edomites, who rejoiced in the destruction of Jerusalem and encouraged further devastation. The psalmist desires justice for the suffering inflicted upon them.

The psalm concludes with a prayer for the destruction of Babylon and a pronouncement of blessing upon those who would repay Babylon for its treatment of the Israelites. The psalmist expresses a deep sense of anger and desire for vengeance, invoking a curse upon the Babylonians.

Psalm 137 portrays the deep anguish and longing of the Israelites during their exile in Babylon. It reflects their sorrow and longing for Jerusalem, their inability to worship and sing songs of praise in their captive state, and their desire for justice against their oppressors. While the concluding verses express intense emotions and desires for retribution, it is important to note that the psalms reflect the personal feelings and experiences of the psalmists and do not necessarily prescribe or endorse such actions.
