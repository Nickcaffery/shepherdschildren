Psalm 129

1. "Greatly have they afflicted me from my youth"— let Israel now say—

2. "Greatly have they afflicted me from my youth, yet they have not prevailed against me.

3. The plowers plowed upon my back; they made long their furrows."

4. The Lord is righteous; he has cut the cords of the wicked.

5. May all who hate Zion be put to shame and turned backward!

6. Let them be like the grass on the housetops, which withers before it grows up,

7. with which the reaper does not fill his hand nor the binder of sheaves his arms,

8. nor do those who pass by say, "The blessing of the Lord be upon you! We bless you in the name of the Lord!"

Commentary

Psalm 129 is a psalm of lament that recounts the afflictions and persecutions endured by Israel, yet expresses confidence in the Lord's deliverance and righteousness. It calls for the shame and defeat of those who hate Zion and affirms God's justice.

The psalm begins by declaring the afflictions that Israel has endured from their youth. It acknowledges the persistent affliction and opposition faced by the nation throughout its history. Despite the afflictions, Israel affirms that their enemies have not been able to overcome them.

The psalmist uses the imagery of plowers who have plowed upon their back, creating long furrows. This imagery depicts the severe and painful oppression and mistreatment endured by Israel.

The psalmist proclaims the righteousness of the Lord, acknowledging His faithfulness and justice. They declare that the Lord has cut the cords of the wicked, symbolizing the severing of the power and influence of those who oppress and afflict Israel.

The psalm concludes with a prayer for the shame and defeat of those who hate Zion, referring to those who are hostile to the people and the city of Jerusalem. The psalmist desires their turning backward, signifying their failure and retreat. They use the imagery of grass on housetops that withers before it can grow, depicting the short-lived nature of the enemies' plans and ambitions.

The psalmist expresses that the enemies' efforts will not yield fruitful results. They will not be able to reap a harvest or receive blessings. The psalmist desires that those who pass by the enemies will not offer them the blessing of the Lord but instead pronounce their own blessing in the name of the Lord.

Psalm 129 expresses the hardships and afflictions endured by Israel throughout its history. It affirms the resilience and confidence of Israel in the face of opposition, trusting in the Lord's righteousness and deliverance. The psalm concludes with a prayer for the shame and defeat of those who oppose Zion, highlighting the ultimate triumph of God's justice and the desire for blessings to be pronounced upon those who are faithful to the Lord.
